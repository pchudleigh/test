1               Project Brief

 

Background and Context

 

The importance of a strong digital presence is now fundamental to all organisations, and in the charity sector this has never been more important than it is today. Maggie’s has developed a variety of areas in the digital space to help engage important stakeholders and parties we are involved with. There are a number of key areas that digital has enabled that remain vital, these include efficient, easy fundraising and donating, news dissemination, information on services and help, campaigning and events, partnerships etc. v

 

Given the change in importance of digital and the recent work undertaken with Pentagram (design agency) to develop a new brand identity, the time is now right to revisit the Maggie’s public web-site, to ensure it is fit for purpose and optimised and to look to incorporate the new visual identity into a new site. This new site should also integrate with our other digital eco-system components (primarily social media platforms and databases) and provide well linked user journeys and easy access to key content.

 

In common with many web-sites we are now experiencing much higher volumes of visits from mobile devices and therefore a mobile responsive design approach will be needed that covers all the basic device protocols.

The participating agencies are asked to give comment to the following in their response.

Key Objectives of a New Maggie’s Web-Site

 

Our key aims for the website redevelopment project are, in priority order:

 

·       Simplify site navigation and functionality
Ability to find your nearest Maggie’s centre, find online cancer information and support, make a donation or regular gift, get involved in fundraising or volunteering, find out more about Maggie’s and access our news and publications. This will mean understanding better the journey’s our site users need to fulfil as well as streamlining and signposting.

 

·       Communicate our charitable status
Clearly communicate that we are a cancer support charity and provide the easiest journey possible to donate (one off and regular gift) or get involved. Better demonstrate the impact donating has on the people supported in our Centres, showing the difference a contribution will make.

 

·       Integrate our cancer support information
Deliver a site structure that brings our cancer information closely onto our main website platform, improving the user experience for people seeking cancer information.  This will support our new content strategy to signpost to the best cancer information provided by others, incorporate content on how the support offered at Maggie’s physical can help and offer content from our expert contributors.

 

·       Support interactivity in delivering online cancer support
Safely and securely deliver our online kitchen table equivalent - peer to peer and facilitated forums, including under 18’s, private messaging and sessional group chat. Simple log in and membership process.

 

·       Centre microsites
Provide Centre specific areas (or microsites) that allow staff in each Centre to manage and publish their own content, from cancer support monthly timetables to local fundraising events, campaigns and social media. Provide a local lens on the bigger national picture. Ensure Centre specific areas are easy to duplicate to support our growth ambitions. 

 

·       Refreshed brand identity
Offer a seamless brand experience, incorporating Maggie’s new brand identity, including look and feel, tone of voice and overall content strategy which supports our repositioning as ‘The Home of Cancer Care.’

 

·       Improved UX elements
Deliver intuitive and personalised user experience that can incorporate geo-location functionality, serves (or signposts) to relevant content based on what the user is searching and viewing and ability to save favourite content.

 

·       Integration with existing databases and social media feeds
Offer seamless integration with our supporter database to migrate personal data and income, specifically when working with third party microsites and suppliers, including Event Management Systems, API’s and fundraising programmes. Also integrate content and links to our relevant social media platforms and sign post to suitable sections of presence on platforms such as Facebook.

Please give consideration to the above and use these objectives to help us ensure we have suitable focus for our limited budgets and resources.

In addition, we have discussed progressing a ‘quick fix’ re-brand of our existing site so our brand identity can be launched whilst the wider website development project is delivered. We would like to understand how this could be approached, the pros and cons and overall cost implications.

3               Background to Maggie’s

3.1          About Maggie’s

A diagnosis of cancer can feel like a kick in the stomach. People can usually recall less than a quarter of what was said when they are told they have cancer. Many are left reeling from the conversation, unable to make sense of the diagnosis and treatment options, struggling to understand how they will cope.  Hospitals can deliver excellent clinical care, yet the emotional and practical support that people with cancer need is often simply not available.

 

Our founder, Maggie Keswick Jencks, recognised this in her own experience with cancer.  In May 1993 she was told that her breast cancer had recurred and spread to her bones, liver and brain. When asked, her oncologist gave her two to three months to live. By joining a trial involving advanced chemotherapy Maggie extended her life by a further 18 months, and it was in this time that her idea for a cancer caring centre was born. She worked closely with Laura Lee, her oncology nurse and now Chief Executive of Maggie’s, and wrote an article for a medical journal on a patient’s perspective on being treated for cancer.

 

Her article ‘A View from the Front Line’ gave her the opportunity to work out what it was that she and the many others affected by cancer needed. She was convinced that everybody would feel better as she did if they felt able to take some active role in what was happening to them.

Maggie died in July 1995, just over a year before the first Maggie’s Centre opened.

 

Since then we have developed a network of 21 Maggie’s Centres across the UK and internationally (Hong Kong, Tokyo) and there are a further nine in development. Our Centres are based in the grounds of NHS hospitals and staffed by experts who offer people the practical, social and the emotional support to help them find their way through cancer. Each centre is unique and is designed by leading architectural firms and we are proud to count Richard Rogers, Zaha Hadid, Frank Gehry, Thomas Heatherwick, Foster and Partners as valued and prestigious contributors.

 

(To give you further context and background we will supply a number of publications as part of the briefing including ‘Architecture of Hope’ a coffee table book guide to the architectural aspects of the centres, our short ‘Architectural Brief’ which guides the architect to how our buildings should make people feel and  our ‘Medical Brief’ which gives an overview of the evidenced based approach to our cancer support).

3.2          ​​About the current Maggie’s website

Maggie’s website (www.maggiescentres.org) was last redeveloped in early 2014. This brought several separate websites under a common content management system (Django CMS) with a simplified information architecture and common look-and-feel. It delivered a national website which positioned Maggie’s and what we offer and our fundraising ideas, but encapsulated local Centre microsites for information about local news, Centre staff, timetables and fundraising.

 

The redeveloped website also brought in the idea of fundraising profiles that fully integrated with JustGiving’s APIs. This allowed visitors to tell us about their fundraising, and ‘advertise’ their events through our website, but with the online fundraising managed by the JustGiving platform.

 

However, there were some websites that remained on external platforms with different look-and-feel, separate navigation and registration, and lying outside the main information architecture thus limiting cross-site search.

 

These websites are described below:

 

●      CancerLinks (cancerlinks.maggiescentres.org)
A gateway to high quality information and support for people with cancer, worried about cancer, or affected in some way by someone else’s cancer. As well as provided our own information about cancer, it signposts to quality cancer information and services across the internet through links curated by our cancer support team. Maggie's CancerLinks helps to guide the way round the huge amount of cancer information on the internet.

●      Donation forms (secure.maggiescentres.org)

Our donation forms are managed through the Baigent donation management system processed by WorldPay. It allows us to manage the layout and fields captured through a rudimentary form management interface. However, Baigent is a legacy system that will soon be retired by operators, WPN Chameleon (www.chameleon.eu).

●      Maggie’s Online Centre (community.maggiescentres.org)
Our Online Centre provides blogs, forums, messaging and group chat for people with cancer. It is staffed by cancer professionals who provide one-to-one support through private messaging, as well as group support through timetabled text chat sessions and regular blogs.

●      Culture Crawl microsite (www.maggiescentres.org/culturecrawl)

Through the CMS we have the ability to create microsites that use the core look-and-feel of our website. An example of this is our Culture Crawl event website – our largest annual fundraising event.

 

●      Event registration (Active Endurance)
We currently use Active Endurance (www.activeendurance.com) to manage registrations to our large fundraising events (eg. Culture Crawl). Our fundraising operations team are looking at other platforms for managing our events, including Event Brite (www.eventbrite.co.uk).

 

●      Tribute Funds (tributefunds.maggiescentres.org)

To support those who wish to remember a loved one who has died of cancer, we offer access to a Tribute Fund platform. This is a white-labelled website from Much Loved (www.muchloved.com).

We are also hoping our revised information architecture will help those looking to donate or fundraise for Maggie’s to easier find ideas and support in doing so. Our revised information architecture is available in our additional background information.

 

Whilst this will form a good start for discussions around information architecture for the site, particularly around getting cancer support, we acknowledge this will need further work as part of this project and we would need IA expertise to help hone it to meet the needs of all our audiences.

4.3.3      ​ Secure engineering principles

The developer shall apply all best practice secure software development practices to avoid and resolve any security issues in the tool; this should be back-ported to other versions of the tool using the same code base. Members of the developer team must be aware of and utilise secure programming techniques and employ due diligence in the design of data models and application code in order to ensure the confidentiality and integrity of the data stored within it. The tool should be designed so as to avoid common web programming security vulnerability including (but not limited to) the OWASP top 10:

 

●      Injection attacks

●      Broken Authentication and Session Management

●      Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)

●      Insecure Direct Object References

●      Security Misconfiguration

●      Sensitive Data Exposure

●      Missing Function Level Access Control

●      Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF)

●      Using Known Vulnerable Components

●      Un-validated Redirects and Forwards

 

Any personal or sensitive data must be encrypted when transiting public networks such as the internet. for example, the use of https for login forms or sites using cookies for authentication.

 

Maggie’s should be notified immediately if any security vulnerabilities or concerns are raised with code running on a publicly available system (live or staging).
